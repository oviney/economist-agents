**Question:** What does the current, credible evidence actually say about whether AI coding assistants (GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, etc.) improve professional software developer productivity — including counter-evidence such as the 2025 METR randomized controlled trial that found experienced open-source developers were slower with AI tools, GitHub's own Copilot studies, DORA/Google findings, and any pe…

**Verification outcome:** 19 confirmed · 6 refuted/unverified · 9 sources.

## The contrarian thesis it supports

Adoption and *perceived* value are near-universal, but the controlled evidence
splits by context and experience — and the sharpest studies find AI can slow
experts down while they *feel* faster. The story is the perception–reality gap,
not a simple yes/no.

## Verified claims

1. **The METR 2025 randomized controlled trial found that experienced open-source developers using AI tools took on average 19% longer to complete tasks, contradicting expectations of a speedup.**  
   > “developers using AI took on average 19% longer”  
   — [domenic.me](https://domenic.me/metr-ai-productivity/) · verified 3-0

2. **Developers in the study subjectively experienced AI as a speedup even though it measurably slowed them down, demonstrating a perception-vs-reality gap in self-assessed productivity.**  
   > “In retrospect, it's not too surprising that AI was a drag on velocity, while subjectively feeling like a speedup.”  
   — [domenic.me](https://domenic.me/metr-ai-productivity/) · verified 3-0

3. **The AI slowdown is attributed to specific conditions -- large existing codebases with established patterns, expert developers working on their own projects, and low AI reliability -- suggesting the result may not generalize to new/greenfield projects or less-experienced developers.**  
   > “I think the differences more likely come from the other factors the study authors highlighted: small new codebases vs. large existing codebases; less-experienced developers vs. project owners and experts; and low AI reliability.”  
   — [domenic.me](https://domenic.me/metr-ai-productivity/) · verified 3-0

4. **In a controlled experiment with 95 professional developers writing an HTTP server in JavaScript, the group using GitHub Copilot completed the task 55% faster than the group without it (avg 1h11m vs 2h41m).**  
   > “55% faster than the developers who didn't use GitHub Copilot”  
   — [github.blog](https://github.blog/news-insights/research/research-quantifying-github-copilots-impact-on-developer-productivity-and-happiness/) · verified 3-0

5. **The 55% speed gain in the controlled experiment was statistically significant at P=.0017, with a 95% confidence interval of [21%, 89%].**  
   > “Statistical significance: P=.0017 ... 95% confidence interval for speed gain: [21%, 89%]”  
   — [github.blog](https://github.blog/news-insights/research/research-quantifying-github-copilots-impact-on-developer-productivity-and-happiness/) · verified 3-0

6. **In a randomized field experiment, developers given access to GitHub Copilot completed about 26% more tasks (26.08% increase in completed tasks) than the control group.**  
   > “access to Copilot increased output — the number of completed weekly tasks — by 26%”  
   — [mit-genai.pubpub.org](https://mit-genai.pubpub.org/pub/v5iixksv) · verified 3-0

7. **Productivity gains were highly heterogeneous by experience: junior and recently-hired developers gained the most (27%–39% more output), while senior developers gained far less (8%–13%). This experience-dependent effect is a key nuance for the productivity debate.**  
   > “recent hires and developers in more junior positions, who increased their output by 27% to 39%. More senior developers saw productivity gains of 8% to 13%.”  
   — [mit-genai.pubpub.org](https://mit-genai.pubpub.org/pub/v5iixksv) · verified 3-0

8. **DORA's 2024 State of DevOps Report (Google Cloud/DORA research program) finds AI adoption increases individual productivity, workflow, and job satisfaction, but simultaneously HARMS software delivery stability and throughput at the team/system level.**  
   > “AI adoption significantly increases individual productivity, flow, and job satisfaction. However, it also negatively impacts software delivery stability and throughput.”  
   — [dora.dev](https://dora.dev/research/2024/dora-report/) · verified 3-0

9. **The 2025 DORA Report, based on a survey of nearly 5,000 technology professionals, found that 90% of respondents use AI at work and more than 80% believe it has increased their productivity.**  
   > “90% of survey respondents report using AI at work... More than 80% believe it has increased their productivity.”  
   — [cloud.google.com](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/announcing-the-2025-dora-report) · verified 3-0

10. **In 2025 DORA observed a positive relationship between AI adoption and both software delivery throughput and product performance, reversing the prior year's finding.**  
   > “Unlike last year, we observe a positive relationship between AI adoption on both software delivery throughput and product performance.”  
   — [cloud.google.com](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/announcing-the-2025-dora-report) · verified 3-0

11. **Despite productivity gains, AI adoption continues to show a negative relationship with software delivery stability, indicating a quality/stability tradeoff.**  
   > “However, AI adoption does continue to have a negative relationship with software delivery stability.”  
   — [cloud.google.com](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/announcing-the-2025-dora-report) · verified 3-0

12. **The DORA 2025 report, based on a survey of nearly 5,000 technology professionals globally, found AI adoption among software development professionals reached 90%, a 14% increase year-over-year.**  
   > “AI adoption among software development professionals has surged to 90%, marking a 14% increase from last year”  
   — [blog.google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/dora-report-2025/) · verified 3-0

13. **Over 80% of DORA 2025 respondents reported that AI has enhanced their individual productivity.**  
   > “Over 80% of respondents indicate that AI has enhanced their productivity”  
   — [blog.google](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/dora-report-2025/) · verified 3-0

14. **In a controlled experiment, developers given GitHub Copilot completed a programming task (implementing an HTTP server in JavaScript) 55.8% faster than the control group without the tool.**  
   > “The treatment group, with access to the AI pair programmer, completed the task 55.8% faster than the control group.”  
   — [arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590) · verified 3-0

15. **A randomized controlled trial found that experienced open-source developers took 19% longer to complete issues when allowed to use AI tools, meaning AI slowed them down rather than speeding them up.**  
   > “When developers are allowed to use AI tools, they take 19% longer to complete issues”  
   — [metr.org](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) · verified 3-0

16. **The study was an RCT of 16 experienced developers across 246 real issues (averaging ~2 hours each), primarily using Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet.**  
   > “We recruited 16 experienced developers ... 246 total ... primarily Cursor Pro with Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet—frontier models at the time of the study”  
   — [metr.org](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) · verified 3-0

17. **There was a large gap between perception and reality: developers expected AI to speed them up by 24%, and even after experiencing a slowdown they still believed AI had sped them up by 20%.**  
   > “Developers expected AI to speed them up by 24%, and even after experiencing the slowdown, they still believed AI had sped them up by 20%”  
   — [metr.org](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) · verified 3-0

18. **A 2025 METR randomized controlled trial found that giving experienced open-source developers access to AI coding tools increased task completion time by 19% (i.e., made them slower), not faster.**  
   > “allowing AI actually increases completion time by 19%”  
   — [arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.09089) · verified 3-0

19. **The METR study exhibited a large perception-reality gap: developers forecast a ~24% time reduction beforehand and still estimated a 20% reduction after completing tasks, while the measured outcome was a 19% slowdown.**  
   > “Before tasks: developers forecast 24% time reduction. After completion: they estimated 20% reduction. Actual result: 19% slowdown—a striking reversal of expectations.”  
   — [arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.09089) · verified 3-0

## Refuted / unverified — DO NOT USE without re-checking

The adversarial step killed these — notably the widely-cited Accenture numbers
(that study was walked back). A naive single-pass researcher would have shipped them.

- ✗ In a survey of over 2,000 developers, more than 90% agreed they complete tasks faster with Copilot, especially repetitive tasks.
- ✗ In a randomized controlled trial with Accenture developers, GitHub Copilot users showed an 8.69% increase in pull requests per developer and a 15% increase in pull request merge rate.
- ✗ The Accenture RCT reported an 84% increase in successful builds among developers using GitHub Copilot.
- ✗ In the DORA 2025 survey, only a minority (59%) reported a positive influence of AI on code quality, indicating self-reported quality gains are less universal than productivity gains.
- ✗ The study was an RCT of 16 experienced open-source developers across 246 randomized tasks, using a within-subjects design.
- ✗ A GitClear analysis of 211 million lines of code written between 2020 and 2024 at major technology companies found increased code duplication, higher short-term churn, and a sharp decline in code reuse, with 'copy/paste'-style duplication outpacing refactored code reuse for the first time — a signal of accelerating technical debt in the AI-assisted coding era.
