Every CFO who has approved an LLM integration has been sold the same reassurance: inference costs are falling, and they will keep falling. This is true. What the slide deck rarely shows is that token consumption tends to expand faster than unit costs decline, producing a cost curve that looks manageable in a proof-of-concept and alarming in production. The companies that built on vague assumptions about "AI costs next to nothing" are now reconciling their cloud bills with considerably more attention. The argument here is not that language models are unaffordable — they are not, for carefully designed applications. The argument is that most companies deploying them have not actually run the unit economics. They have optimised for capability and deferred the accounting to a later conversation. That later conversation has arrived, and it is arriving in the form of invoices.

## A Falling Price and a Rising Appetite

OpenAI has cut the price of GPT-4-class inference by roughly an order of magnitude since the model's commercial launch, and Google's Gemini pricing follows a similarly aggressive downward trajectory. Sam Altman, OpenAI's chief executive, has publicly framed declining inference costs as the engine of AI adoption. He is not wrong. But the frame elides a structural problem: when you make something dramatically cheaper, demand does not merely grow — it transforms. Features that were once gated behind cost constraints get unlocked. Prompts that were once terse become verbose. Pipelines that once called the model once per user action start calling it a dozen times. Klarna, the Swedish payments company, became a celebrated case study in AI-driven cost reduction after deploying a customer-service chatbot that it claimed handled the workload of 700 human agents. What attracted less attention was the infrastructure cost of serving millions of conversations through a large language model — a cost that scales directly with conversation length and volume. Klarna's headline numbers were impressive; the unit economics of individual interactions were not always discussed with the same precision. This is the pattern: companies announce AI deployments in terms of headcount avoided or time saved. They are quieter about the token bills.

## The Multiplier Hidden in Every System Prompt

A single user query to an LLM-powered product rarely generates a single API call. Engineers at production AI companies — Cohere, Mistral, and Anthropic's enterprise customers among them — routinely describe architectures in which a single user action triggers three to ten model invocations: one to classify intent, one or more to retrieve context, one to generate a draft, and one to evaluate or rewrite the output. Each invocation carries a system prompt, which may itself run to several thousand tokens. The chart below illustrates how token costs compound across a multi-step pipeline: a user query of 50 tokens can produce a billed session exceeding 15,000 tokens once context windows, chain-of-thought steps, and evaluation passes are included. This is not wasteful engineering — it is often necessary for quality. But it is a multiplier that most cost projections do not account for, and the gap between a back-of-envelope estimate and a real production bill can be an order of magnitude. Anthropic's published documentation on its Claude API explicitly notes that caching system prompts can reduce costs substantially, but prompt caching requires deliberate architectural choices that teams building quickly rarely make. The default path is also the expensive one.

## Caching as a Financial Discipline

Prompt caching has emerged as one of the most effective — and most underused — tools in the production LLM cost stack. Anthropic introduced prompt caching for Claude in 2024, charging a fraction of the standard input token rate for cached prefixes. OpenAI followed with a similar mechanism for GPT-4o. Yet adoption remains uneven. Engineers at many companies build their first production LLM features without understanding the caching mechanics, then optimise later — after the invoice has provided its own form of education. Stripe's engineering culture, documented in its internal developer guides, treats cost as a first-class metric alongside latency and error rate; this discipline is unusual rather than standard in the industry. Most teams treat cost as someone else's problem until it becomes everyone's problem. The financial logic of running models in production increasingly resembles database engineering: indices, caching strategies, and query patterns matter enormously, and the teams that treat the model as a black box to be called freely will pay for the privilege.

## The Coming Reckoning Among Model Vendors

The pricing strategies of the major model vendors are not sustainable in their current form. OpenAI, which operates at a loss measured in billions of dollars annually, subsidises inference pricing as a growth strategy. Anthropic, backed by substantial investment from Google and Amazon, does the same. This subsidy transfers an enormous amount of cost from AI-using companies to AI-providing companies — a transfer that will eventually need to be renegotiated, probably abruptly and probably at an inconvenient moment for whoever has built the thinnest margin product on top of it. When pricing normalises toward the true cost of compute, the applications built on the assumption of permanently cheap inference will face a reckoning. The winners will be the teams that understood the unit economics in 2025 and designed accordingly: short system prompts, aggressive caching, model routing that sends simple queries to cheaper models and reserves large context windows for tasks that genuinely require them. The analogy to the early cloud era is instructive but imperfect. AWS eventually raised prices on several services as demand matured and the era of loss-leader infrastructure ended. AI inference costs will likely keep falling in absolute terms — but the gap between "falling prices" and "acceptable unit economics for a specific product" is exactly where the next wave of AI startup failures will quietly cluster. A token is cheap. A thousand tokens, multiplied by ten API calls, multiplied by ten million users, multiplied by a system prompt that nobody trimmed in eighteen months, is something else entirely.
