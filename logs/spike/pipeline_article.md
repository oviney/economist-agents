Ask a software engineer at GitHub or Microsoft whether Copilot has made her faster, and she will say yes without hesitation. Ask her director whether the team is shipping features any faster, and the reply will be a long, awkward pause. Bosses paying nineteen dollars a head per month for Copilot Business want to know where the dividend has gone. That gap — between buoyant individual perception and stubborn organisational throughput — is the productivity paradox of AI coding assistants in miniature. These tools are genuinely useful. Output from the teams that use them is, by the most rigorous measures available, only modestly improved, and on some squads it is plainly worse. Models are not the culprit. Writing code was never the binding constraint, and assistants that make typing easier have quietly shifted the workload to places where they do not help at all.

## Stopwatches and self-reports

Researchers have built a small industry on the gap between what developers feel and what their managers measure. A 2025 study with the unimprovable title "My productivity is boosted, but.." interviewed engineers about Copilot, Cursor and their kin, and discovered an enthusiasm bordering on infatuation paired with a long list of grievances. Suggestions arrive at lightning speed but require careful policing. Accepting code one did not write carries a real cognitive cost. Engineers report saving time on boilerplate and losing it on second-guessing. A larger enterprise study published the same year, examining hundreds of professional users of an in-house coding assistant, found a similar pattern. Keystrokes saved were real. Aggregate effects on time-to-ship, defect density and review-queue length were harder to detect, and in several cases pointed the wrong way. The 2026 paper "Beyond the Commit" goes further still, arguing that metrics most flattering to AI assistants — characters generated, suggestions accepted — are precisely those that correlate worst with shipped, working software. This is the awkward shape of the evidence. Developers are happier. The pull-request graph is not.

## The migrating bottleneck

Software production is a queueing system. Code is written, then reviewed, then merged, then tested in staging, then deployed, then watched. For two decades the typing step has been a small fraction of the total. Empirical work going back to Microsoft Research's own studies of its engineers placed coding at perhaps a quarter of a developer's day; the rest is meetings, reading other people's code, debugging, waiting for builds and writing tests. An assistant that doubles the speed of the smallest stage cannot double the throughput of the whole. Worse, it can lengthen the queue downstream. Generated code arrives faster than it can be reviewed. Reviewers, faced with longer diffs of unfamiliar provenance, slow down to compensate. Test suites groan under the new volume. Junior engineers, deprived of the slow apprenticeship of writing tedious code by hand, struggle to debug what their tools have produced. Diana Larsen, the veteran agile consultant, has long argued that local optimisations in software teams migrate the constraint rather than removing it. Anyone who has read W. Edwards Deming will recognise the pattern: improving a non-bottleneck step rarely improves the system, and frequently degrades it. Copilot is the most expensive demonstration of that thesis to date. As the chart below illustrates, the gulf between self-reported speed-ups and measured cycle-time improvements widens as teams scale. Solo hobbyists report the largest gains and see them; large enterprise squads report the largest gains and see almost none. Tools help the writer. They do not help the system in which the writer is embedded.

## Brynjolfsson's ghost

None of this would surprise Erik Brynjolfsson. His 2017 paper, "Artificial Intelligence and the Modern Productivity Paradox", remains the canonical account of why transformative technologies show up everywhere except the productivity statistics. New general-purpose tools, he argued, require complementary investments — in process, in skills, in organisational redesign — before their gains arrive in the national accounts. Electricity took forty years to reorganise the factory. Computers spent two decades looking like an expensive disappointment before the late-1990s acceleration arrived. Coding assistants are following the same script with eerie fidelity. Capability dazzles. Complementary investments — better review pipelines, AI-aware testing strategies, training curricula that teach engineers to interrogate generated code rather than rubber-stamp it — are barely under way. GitHub's own metrics teams, to their credit, have begun publishing throughput figures alongside the more flattering acceptance rates. Most companies have not. Dashboards that justify the licence renewal still measure the wrong thing. There is a second, sharper possibility. Coding assistants may, like spreadsheets before them, eventually produce a decisive jump in output — but only after the profession reorganises itself around them, which means after a great deal of mediocre code has been merged and a great many junior engineers have been taught the wrong habits. Resolution would arrive on Brynjolfsson's schedule. The bill, in technical debt and atrophied judgement, would arrive separately. For now the safest forecast is also the dullest. Copilot's bosses will keep paying. Copilot's users will keep grinning. And somewhere in the middle, a tired engineering manager will stare at her cycle-time chart and wonder why the future, having arrived, looks so much like the present with a faster autocomplete.
